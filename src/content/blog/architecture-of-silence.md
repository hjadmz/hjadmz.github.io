I don't build minimalist interfaces because they look clean. I build them because the alternative makes me unable to work.

## The nervous-system tax

A cluttered interface charges a tax. Each non-essential affordance costs a fraction of attention to ignore. For most users, that fraction rounds to zero. For a neurodivergent user, it doesn't — the tax compounds, and by the time you've navigated three menus the working memory is gone.

The fix isn't meditation or time-blocking. It's building systems that don't charge the tax in the first place.

## What silence looks like

Silence in a UI is absence of the things that earn nothing. A gradient that doesn't reinforce hierarchy. A rounded card around text that already had hierarchy. A tooltip that says what the label already said. A color that "makes it pop" but carries no information.

Remove them. What's left is a system that gets out of the way.

## Monastic, not minimalist

"Minimalist" has been thoroughly hollowed out as an aesthetic genre — white space, a sans-serif, a photograph of hands. That's a style. Silence is a rule: **every visible element must justify its existence by the function it carries.** If you can delete it and the function is unchanged, delete it.

## The one exception

Silence is not absence of feedback. Every interactive thing still gives you the handshake — focus ring, hover state, motion confirmation. The silence is about ambient noise, not signal noise. The system stays quiet until you ask it a question; then it answers promptly, once, and shuts up again.
